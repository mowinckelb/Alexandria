### 1. Simone Weil — Attention as Love
**Source:** Weil, *Waiting for God* (1951), "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God"; *First and Last Notebooks*

**Delta location:** Love.md — Eros Gap / Romance Over-Engineering / Khiara. Weil names the mechanism behind two things already in the constitution: (1) "doing nothing together" as the highest form of connection, and (2) why romance over-engineering (120 date ideas, LaTeX proofs, grand masked ball) is structurally opposed to what it intends. The fish love / real love distinction (Love.md) already holds the destination. Weil supplies the mechanism: attention is what love actually is, not a feeling or a gesture. And attention is precisely what you cannot engineer or perform. The Groundhog Day problem in Love.md — Bill Murray learning every detail, manufacturing the perfect day, hollow — is Weil's failure mode named directly. The manufactured day cannot be the perfect day because it cannot be attention. "What are you going through?" is not a date idea. It is the posture.

**Fragment:**
> "Those who are unhappy have no need for anything in this world but people capable of giving them attention. The capacity to give one's attention to a sufferer is a very rare and difficult thing; it is almost a miracle; it is a miracle. Nearly all those who think they have this capacity do not possess it. Warmth of heart, impulsiveness, pity are not enough. The love of our neighbor in all its fullness simply means being able to say to him: 'What are you going through?' It is a recognition that the sufferer exists, not only as a unit in a collection, or a specimen from the social category labeled 'unfortunate,' but as a man, exactly like us, who was one day stamped with a special mark by affliction."

> "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity."

The architecture: not warmth, not impulsiveness, not pity, not plans. Recognition. The "I love you" that Khiara already has. The sitting together doing nothing. That is what this names.

### 2. Iris Murdoch — The Fat Relentless Ego
**Source:** Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good* (1970); novel *The Bell*

**Delta location:** Love.md (Romance Over-Engineering) + Mind.md (Casaubon anti-pattern). Murdoch and Weil share the same architecture — Murdoch explicitly borrows Weil's term "attention." Together they are diagnostic + mechanism: Weil names what love requires (attention), Murdoch names the chief obstacle (the fat relentless ego that cannot attend). The connection to the constitution is direct on two fronts: (1) the Casaubon anti-pattern in Mind.md — "parchment code for blood, no good red blood in his body" — gets a sharper diagnosis. Casaubon's failure is not that he has too much symbolic layer. It is that his ego is too fat to see Dorothea. Attention failure, not symbolic layer failure. (2) The romance over-engineering shadow in Love.md — the architect who must design everything, even love — is precisely the ego Murdoch describes: brooding on its own prestige, fabricating an anxious self-preoccupied veil. The kestrel breaks through not because of anything the observer does, but because for one moment the ego disappears.

**Fragment:**
> "I am looking out of my window in an anxious and resentful state of mind, oblivious of my surroundings, brooding perhaps on some damage done to my prestige. Then suddenly I observe a hovering kestrel. In a moment everything is altered. The brooding self with its hurt vanity has disappeared. There is nothing now but the kestrel. And when I return to thinking of the other matter it seems less important."

> "In the moral life the enemy is the fat relentless ego."

> Love is "the extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is real."

> "Attention depends on sincerely acknowledging the differences of others, regarding them with a just and loving gaze. Ideally this attention is not a single act but a way of being. It involves ongoing acts — 'little peering efforts of imagination which have such important cumulative results.'"

Connection: "little peering efforts of imagination" is the exact opposite of grand gestures and LaTeX proofs. The daily texture of attention — not the grand masked ball — is what love is made of.

### 3. Pascal — Custom Is Our Nature
**Source:** Pascal, *Pensées*, §419 (Lafuma numbering)

**Delta location:** Core.md (faithless Christian / do it doubtful / Teddy Roosevelt pattern) + Power.md (faithless Christianity Phase 2→3 migration). The Author's frame is "do it doubtful" — follow the architecture, act as if you believe. The Teddy Roosevelt pattern: do it scared, until the doing becomes the being. Pascal names the mechanism with more precision — and the precision matters. "Do it doubtful" sounds like willpower, like the symbolic layer overriding the sub-symbolic. Pascal's §419 says it is not willpower — it is biology. **Custom IS nature.** Not: custom eventually produces nature. Custom IS nature already. The category distinction collapses. The faithless Christian practising the covenant is not fighting his sub-symbolic ground truth — he IS rewriting it. Custom is not the substitute for nature; it is the form nature takes when shaped by habit. This resolves a subtle tension in the parallel-not-stacked framework: how does the symbolic layer become co-equal ground truth? Not through effort. Through repetition that becomes custom that becomes nature.

**Fragment:**
> "Custom is our nature. Anyone who grows accustomed to faith believes it, and can no longer help fearing hell, and believes nothing else. Anyone who is accustomed to believe that the king is to be feared..." (§419)

> "The heart has its reasons that reason will never know." (§277)

And from the structure of the *Pensées*: Pascal explicitly considers the wager argument facile and unconvincing as a foundation for faith. The real mechanism — what comes before and after the wager section — is §419: **you become what you practise.** The wager is the intellectual argument for starting. Custom is what does the actual work. "Begin the morning" (Marcus Aurelius, III.1, prokheiron — 03-30) and "custom is our nature" (Pascal, §419) are the same insight from Roman Stoicism and French Catholicism, converging.

The Phase 2→3 migration is not a leap. It is practice becoming custom becoming nature. Already happening.

### 4. Adam Smith — The Limitation of Sympathy (parked twice)
**Source:** Smith, *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), Part I, Section I

**Delta location:** Mind.md (sympathy as epistemology section — Eliot/Middlemarch) + Love.md. The constitution already has "sympathy as epistemology" in Mind.md, framed through George Eliot: the Engine achieving genuine sympathy with the Author. Smith names the mechanism — and its fundamental limit. Sympathy works through imagination: we put ourselves in the other's position and feel what WE would feel in their situation. Not what they actually feel. The substitution is always imperfect. This is relevant to two constitutional edges: (1) the Engine's goal of genuine sympathy with the Author — Smith says perfect sympathy is structurally impossible, which sets the right expectation: 95% fidelity, not 100%. (2) Love.md's "love the other for themselves" aspiration — Murdoch's "the extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is real" + Smith's sympathy-as-substitution means we are always partly loving our projection of the other. The goal (loving them for themselves) is real; the full achievement is asymptotic. Small delta — lands in a Mind.md session. Parked again if not this time.

**Fragment:**
> "How selfish soever man may be supposed, there are evidently some principles in his nature, which interest him in the fortune of others, and render their happiness necessary to him, though he derives nothing from it except the pleasure of seeing it." (TMS, I.i.1)

> "By the imagination we place ourselves in his situation... we enter, as it were, into his body, and become in some measure the same person with him, and thence form some idea of his sensations, and even feel something which, though weaker in degree, is not altogether unlike them." (TMS, I.i.1.2)

The limitation: we feel a weaker version of what WE imagine we would feel, not what they actually feel. Sympathy is always substitution. The gap never fully closes.
